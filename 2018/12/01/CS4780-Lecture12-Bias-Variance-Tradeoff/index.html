CS 4780 Lecture 12: Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Posted on 2018-12-01 | In CS 4780 Study Notes

Definitions

By decomposition of expected test error, we have: $$E_{x, y, D}[(h_{D}(x)-y)^2] = E_{x, D}[(h_{D}(x)-\bar h(x))^2]+E_{x,y}[(\bar y(x)-y)^2] + E_{x}[(\bar h(x)-\bar y(x))^2]$$

The first part is variance: it captures how much your classifier changes if you train on a different training set. How “over-specialized” is your classifier to a particular training set (overfitting)? If we have the best possible model for our training data, how far off are we from the average classifier?

The second part is noise: This error measures ambiguity due to your data distribution and feature representation. You can never beat this, it is an aspect of the data.

The third part is bias: What is the inherent error that you obtain from your classifier even with infinite training data? This is due to your classifier being “biased” to a particular kind of solution (e.g. linear classifier). In other words, bias is inherent to your model.

Detecting and Fixing High-Variance and High-Bias

The graph above plots the training error and the test error and can be divided into two overarching regimes. In the first regime, training error is below the desired error threshold, but test error is significantly higher. In the second regime, test error is remarkably close to training error, but both are above the desired tolerance.

Regime 1

Symptoms
  1. Training error much lower than testing error.
  2. Training error lower than $\epsilon$.
  3. Testing error higher than $\epsilon$.
Remedies
  1. Add more training data.
  2. Reduce model-complexity (more complicated model tends to overfit, thus prone to high variance)
  3. Bagging

Regime 2

Symptoms
  1. Training error close to testing error.
  2. Both training error and testing error higher than $\epsilon$.
Remedies
  1. Use more complex models.
  2. Add more features.
  3. Boosting
